Accessible Components: Dropdown Sort

Have you ever been online shopping and needed to sort items from low to high prices, or find the best-rated products? You click on a dropdown menu, expecting it to work seamlessly—but sometimes, it doesn’t. Maybe you can’t navigate it with your keyboard, or the options feel clunky on your phone.

Dropdown menus are small but powerful components in user interfaces. They help us organize and filter content, but if they’re not designed well, they can become frustrating barriers.

This case study takes a deep dive into how different platforms—Amazon, Depop, and Sephora—handle dropdown sorting. We'll explore their usability, accessibility, and design choices to see what works, what doesn’t, and how they could be improved.

Dropdowns might seem simple, but the way we interact with them—whether by clicking, tapping, or using a keyboard—can make or break a user’s experience. Let's begin by looking at input methods: how these menus behave when using a mouse, keyboard, or touchscreen across different platforms.

Part 1: Input

Dropdown menus are essential UI components that allow users to make selections from a list of options. A well-designed dropdown must support multiple input methods, including mouse, keyboard, and touch interactions.

Below is an analysis of how dropdown menus function in Sephora, Depop, and Amazon, addressing their usability across different input methods.

Note: In each screenshot, the dropdown states are highlighted with a red border for visibility! These borders are not actually part of the original interface.

Overall, Amazon's dropdown is highly functional and maintains clearnability, memorability, and efficiency for desktop users. The mobile experience, however, could be more intuitive.

Understanding how users interact with dropdown menus is just one part of the equation— how these menus respond is just as important. A dropdown should clearly indicate when an option has been selected, what is currently active, and whether any changes have taken effect.

Amazon and Sephora at least attempt to provide some form of feedback—whether it’s a highlighted selection or an updated button label. But Depop? It leaves users guessing. Selections don’t always update visibly, keyboard users are left behind entirely, and the checkbox behavior feels unintuitive.

In the next section, we’ll examine how these dropdowns communicate state changes—or fail to do so. We’ll look at visual cues, screen reader support, and interactive feedback to determine which platforms guide users effectively and which ones create unnecessary friction.

Redesigning Depop's Sort Dropdown

Depop’s current dropdown has several usability issues, including a lack of keyboard support, unclear selection feedback, and checkbox behavior that misleads users into thinking multiple selections are possible. Our redesign enhances clarity, efficiency, and accessibility by replacing checkboxes with radio buttons, improving selection feedback, and aligning with Depop’s existing UI patterns.

Below, the first image displays the original dropdown in both its default and clicked states. The second image showcases the revised version with annotations detailing the improvements.

In making these changes, we also considered intentional trade-offs. One key decision was removing checkboxes in favor of radio buttons. While checkboxes could allow users to see multiple options at a glance, they created confusion about whether multiple selections were possible. The redesign opts for clarity over flexibility, ensuring users immediately understand that only one option can be selected at a time. Additionally, while keeping the dropdown open after selection might reduce friction for some users, it led to uncertainty about whether changes were applied. The redesign closes the dropdown upon selection, providing a clearer indication that the sorting action has taken effect.

Depop’s dropdown redesign highlights an important reality of e-commerce: small design choices can have a big impact. From missing feedback to misleading checkboxes, minor usability issues added up to create an experience that was frustrating rather than seamless.

When navigation isn't intuitive, users hesitate. When accessibility is overlooked, users are excluded. And when a simple sort feature creates confusion, it disrupts the entire shopping flow. In a competitive e-commerce space, these friction points can be the difference between conversion and abandonment.

Our redesign demonstrates that thoughtful, incremental changes—like improving contrast, refining button states, and aligning with platform-wide patterns—can boost usability, efficiency, and accessibility without a complete overhaul. These principles don’t just apply to Depop’s dropdown; they are relevant to every interface in digital retail.

Part 4: Reflection

Depop’s original dropdown suffered from unclear selection behavior, misleading checkboxes, and no keyboard support, making it difficult to use for anyone who wasn’t clicking with a mouse. The revised design addresses these issues by replacing checkboxes with radio buttons (which more clearly indicate single-selection behavior), improving contrast, and ensuring consistency with Depop’s existing UI patterns. These changes make sorting more intuitive, efficient, and accessible for all users.

One of the biggest accessibility gaps in the original design was the lack of keyboard navigation. Without it, users who rely on screen readers or tab-based interactions couldn’t use the dropdown at all. The redesign introduces keyboard support, higher contrast between selected and unselected options, and clearer visual indicators—all of which remove friction for users with motor impairments, low vision, or cognitive disabilities.

Kat Holmes describes disability as a "mismatched interaction between the features of a person's body and the features of the environment in which they live." The original Depop dropdown created this exact kind of mismatch by assuming all users can easily click with a mouse. Anyone who couldn’t—whether due to motor limitations, vision impairments, or personal preference—was effectively shut out. The redesign closes this gap by supporting multiple input methods, ensuring clear visual feedback, and making the dropdown usable for a broader range of people.

This idea of mismatches is everywhere in UI design. A website that relies only on hover effects creates a mismatch for touchscreen users who can’t hover. A form that requires a mouse click but doesn’t support the keyboard’s "Enter" key creates a mismatch for users who navigate with a keyboard. Every design choice either increases or decreases these mismatches—and inclusive design is about actively reducing them so that more people can interact with technology on their own terms.

- Amazon’s keyboard navigation allows users to sort items using arrow keys and Enter—removing barriers for screen reader users and improving efficiency for anyone who prefers the keyboard.
- Depop’s lack of selection feedback creates a mismatch for users with cognitive disabilities, who rely on clear, confirming indicators to know their action was successful.

Mouse and touch interactions tend to be prioritized in UI development because they’re the most common. But this often comes at the expense of keyboard and screen reader users, creating unintended exclusion. When sorting through products, a user should never have to wonder if their selection was registered or struggle to navigate a dropdown because it wasn’t designed for them.

At the core of this redesign is the idea that small design choices can have a huge impact. A dropdown might seem like a tiny detail, but in e-commerce, where friction directly affects engagement and sales, accessibility isn’t just about compliance—it’s about creating a better experience for everyone. The more we recognize mismatches and design around them, the more we move toward a digital world that includes, rather than excludes, by default.
